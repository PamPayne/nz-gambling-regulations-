
# Online Casino Gambling Bill 2025: Policy Approval for Regulations

## Portfolio
Internal Affairs

## Purpose
This paper seeks approval to develop regulations to enable the implementation of the Online Casino Gambling Bill 2025 (the Bill)

## Previous Decisions
In June 2025, the Cabinet Legislation Committee approved the Bill for introduction [LEG-25-MIN-0124].
In September 2025, the Cabinet Business Committee noted that an earlier version of this paper had been withdrawn [CBC-25-MIN-0051].

## Proposal
The Bill establishes a licensing system for online casino gambling to facilitate a safer and compliant regulated online casino gambling market. The system is expected to come into force on 1 July 2026.
Regulations are required to implement the Bill’s cost recovery arrangements and key aspects of the licensing compliance conditions. A phased approach to the development of the regulations is proposed.
This paper focuses on the first set of regulations, relating to:
 harm prevention and minimisation;
 consumer protection and record-keeping;
 advertising and marketing (further details are in Appendix B);
 fees, levies or charges for cost recovery

## Impact Analysis
A Regulatory Impact Statement (RIS) is in Appendix A. Officials consider that the RIS meets the quality assurance criteria

## Financial Implications
he establishment and ongoing costs of operating the regulatory system are to be recovered from licensed online casino gambling operators. The scheme’s ongoing costs are estimated to be in the range of 9(2)(f)(iv) per annum.

## Legislative  Implications and  Timing
The Bill, which is currently before the House, is intended to commence on 1 July 2026. Regulations need to be promulgated by that date.

DIA will keep stakeholders informed about the progress of the regulations.

# Online Casino Gambling Bill 2025: approval to develop regulations

## Proposal
1. I seek Cabinet’s approval of policy proposals, and to issue drafting instructions to the Parliamentary Counsel Office, for the development of regulations that will enable the implementation of the Online Casino Gambling Bill 2025.

## Executive Summary
2. The Online Casino Gambling Bill 2025 (the Bill) was introduced to Parliament in June 2025. The Bill establishes a licensing system for online casino gambling to facilitate a safer and compliant regulated online casino gambling market
3. The Bill prohibits the conduct and advertisement of unlicensed online casino gambling, prevents and minimises harm, limits opportunities for crime and dishonesty, and provides protections for consumers within the regulated online casino gambling market.
4. I anticipate the Bill will receive Royal Assent in December 2025, and will come into force on 1 July 2026. The Bill requires regulations to be developed to implement cost recovery arrangements and key aspects of the licensing compliance conditions. Approval to draft
regulations is needed now to ensure that they are ready as soon as practicable so that online casino gambling operators are aware of the required obligations, should they choose to compete for a licence
5. I propose a phased approach to developing regulations. This paper addresses the core regulations covering harm prevention and minimisation, consumer protection and recordkeeping, advertising and marketing, and fees, levies or charges to enable cost recovery.
6. The proposed regulations take account of feedback received from a targeted engagement process conducted by the Department of Internal Affairs (the Department) earlier this year.
7. Subject to Cabinet’s agreement, I seek approval of the policy proposals and to issue drafting instructions to the Parliamentary Counsel Office with the intention that the regulations be completed as soon as possible following the Bill’s commencement.

## Background
The development of regulations will ensure the competitive licensing process can get underway in a timely manner and the regulatory system can be stood up in 2026
8. The Bill enables regulations to be developed to ensure licensed operators’ compliance requirements are clearly established, critical functions for regulatory management can be put in place and operators are well placed to complete the licensing process.
9. I am proposing a phased approach to the development of the regulations. The first set of regulations – which are the focus of this paper – cover:
9.1 harm prevention and minimisation (harm minimisation);
9.2 consumer protection and record-keeping (consumer protection);
9.3 advertising and marketing (advertising); and
9.4 fees, levies or charges for cost recovery.
10. The Bill enables further regulations to be made on a range of other matters. These regulations will be developed on an as required basis following the commencement of the Bill. As the online casino market develops there may also be a need to
refine the parameters of the online gambling regulatory system as provided for in clause 79(1)(a) and (b) of the Bill, which enable regulations to refine the definitions of online casino gambling or online casino gambling technology.
11. The Bill also enables minimum standards relating to online casino gambling technology to be prescribed by the Secretary for Internal Affairs (the Secretary). The Department is working on these standards in consultation with targeted stakeholder groups. The standards will be finalised and implemented in tandem with the regulations.

## Regulations take account of targeted stakeholder engagement
Harm minimisation, consumer protection, and advertising
12. The Department sought feedback on the proposed regulations in two rounds. It conducted preliminary consultation with targeted stakeholders through workshops in February 2025, and in June 2025 the Department provided a consultation paper on policy options to
targeted stakeholders. This included online and land-based gambling operators, gambling harm treatment service providers, media companies, researchers, gambling technology providers and industry regulatory bodies (including the Advertising Standards Authority).
13. Overall, stakeholders were supportive of the proposed approach but raised some concerns about how requirements would be effectively implemented and enforced. Many gambling operators advocated for regulations that would maximise channelling customers to licensed operators, whereas gambling harm treatment service providers and researchers primarily focussed on how well proposals prevented and minimised gambling harm. The regulatory proposals in this paper take account of these differing perspectives and, in my view, strike an appropriate balance.
Cost recovery
14. In May 2025, the Department circulated a consultation paper on cost recovery policy options to targeted stakeholder groups. The submissions expressed broad support for recovering the regulator’s full costs from operators. Most suggested some minor refinements to the proposed cost recovery model.
15. Reflecting on the stakeholder feedback, the regulatory proposals were modified to clarify requirements and provide greater certainty to regulated parties around the timing and proposed triggers for cost recovery reviews.

## Proposed regulations
An initial set of regulations will be put in place to prescribe core compliance requirements as set out in the Bill and to establish cost recovery arrangements
16. The Bill contains a number of clauses that enable regulations to be made. Further detail on my proposed measures is set out in the Regulatory Impact Statement (RIS) at Appendix A.

## Harm prevention and minimisation (clause 75)
17. I have sought to strike a balance between effectively preventing and minimising harm, and ensuring proposals are not so restrictive that they limit choice for consumers, which could cause more people to gamble in the black market and increase harm.
18. I propose the regulations incorporate the following requirements:
18.1 Operators must offer players the ability to set limits on their gambling and ensure the process for players to exclude themselves on the operator’s licensed platform is easily identifiable, accessible and as frictionless as possible;
18.2 Operators must exclude those identified as a problem gambler, and those they identify as a problem gambler, from gambling on all licensed online casino platforms controlled by the operator;
18.3 Operators will be required to participate in a centralised self-exclusion register, if an appropriate one is made available;
18.4 The use of bonuses and inducements will be subject to restrictions;
18.5 Operators must offer players the ability to set the frequency of breaks in play and the frequency of personalised pop-up alerts that provide information on their current gambling session, on a per-platform, opt-out basis;
18.6 Operators must have policies to monitor, identify and assist those experiencing gambling harm and include consistent and specific harm minimisation messaging in all online casino gambling advertisements;
18.7 Operators must offer players the ability to limit their own access to what games they are able to play based on the stake limit (minimum and maximum) of those games;
18.8 Game designs and user interface features must be designed to prevent extended, continuous and impulsive play and facilitate low risk play behaviour and prohibit the use of autoplay;
18.9 Network progressive jackpots must only be linked between branded online casinos that are controlled by licensed operators; and,
18.10 Players are limited to playing one online slot game at any one time.

## Consumer protection and record-keeping (clause 76)
19. In the online gambling environment, operators require consumers’ personal information, proof of identity and financial transaction information, which is often debit cards and bank accounts. Robust consumer protection requirements will give consumers more confidence to engage with licensed operators and switch to the regulated market.
20. I propose the regulations incorporate the following requirements:
20.1 Operators must retain all records related to a consumer’s account information until seven years after the business relationship ends;
20.2 Operators must collect and verify a consumer’s identity at the account creation stage before an account holder is able to make a deposit and determine whether the person has self-excluded themselves from gambling;
20.3 Operators must prohibit the use of credit contracts to pay for online gambling, including the use of credit cards and buy now pay later facilities;
20.4 Players shall only be able to hold one account per platform, and account holders must register a deposit method at the account creation stage and may only register one deposit method at any one time;
20.5 Prior to placing a bet, consumers must be provided with sufficient information to make informed decisions;
20.6 Game designs and user interface features must be clear and not mislead the player. Games shall remove and add credits accurately and according to game rules, and credit a player’s account completely and immediately on winning;
20.7 Operators must provide information and services in languages commonly spoken in New Zealand and must communicate in a form, language and manner that enables the consumer to understand the information provided. Where necessary and practicable, this includes providing access to a competent interpreter;
20.8 Operators must prevent collusion, cheating and software programs to automatically participate in game play (automated gambling); and 
20.9 Player funds must be protected and available for withdrawal on request. Information on current player account balance and playing history should be easily accessible on the platform, and a player must be able to close an account and withdraw remaining funds without undue delay.
21. These requirements will ensure operators have sufficient measures in place to prevent underage online casino gambling and to ensure that consumers are provided with sufficient access to information, and will limit practices that can be misleading or promote risky play.
22. I am proposing to prohibit the use of credit contracts (e.g. credit cards and buy now pay later), as gambling on credit can lead to financial hardship and debt which can cause significant distress1. It is best to treat credit cards and buy now pay later providers
consistently, as both payment types are subject to the Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003. Prohibiting one and not the other could distort market competition.

## Advertising and marketing (clause 77)
24. My proposed advertising package aligns with Cabinet’s previous direction to prohibit sponsorship and the legislative requirement under clause 77(3) of the Bill that requires the Minister of Internal Affairs to have regard to the need to protect children from being harmed
by online casino gambling.
25. Advertising plays a key role in a successful licensing system by channelling players to the regulated market. However, advertising can also normalise gambling and incite excessive play.
26. I have developed a package of advertising proposals in line with clause 77 that will:
26.1 Restrict the permissible audience that online casino advertisements may target or reasonably appeal to, and the forms of advertising operators are permitted to use to those explicitly permitted in the regulations;
26.2 Prohibit the use of paid endorsements and affiliate marketing (including social media influencers), and provide that operators are liable for any breach of the regulations by a third party they contract with (e.g. a marketing firm);
26.3 Allow advertising of bonuses and inducements with restrictions;
26.4 Set clear, universal restrictions that will apply across all forms of advertising, including limiting the use of personalised and interactive advertisements; and
26.5 Restrict the content of advertisements and promotions to prevent harmful and misleading content, including prohibiting the advertising of jackpots.
27. A full list of the proposed advertising regulations can be found in Appendix B. I am confident that the proposed advertising package will minimise the risk of vulnerable groups being exposed to online casino gambling advertising, especially children. The package is designed to limit or restrict harmful advertising while still enabling licensed operators to use a wide range of advertising tools. This will balance the core objectives of the regulatory system with the need to effectively channel consumers to the regulated market.

29. The regulator will assess the ability of an operator to comply with these requirements when the operator submits their licence application and will contribute to determining whether a licence is granted. The regulator will also monitor compliance on an ongoing basis.

## Cost recovery (clause 78)
The Bill’s requirements, previous Cabinet decisions and well-defined cost recovery principles have framed the approach to cost recovery
30. Clause 78 of the Bill prescribes the setting of fees, levies, or charges to enable the recovery of the direct and indirect cost of the Secretary in:
30.1 publicising and informing people about the requirements of the Bill;
30.2 administering, enforcing, and monitoring compliance with the Bill; and
30.3 doing anything else authorised or required by the Bill.
31. Cabinet has previously agreed that:
31.1 the tagged $20 million contingency provided for the establishment of the licensed online casino gambling model is to be fully repaid to the Crown from licensed operators via fees within a ten-year capital forecast period (CAB-24-MIN-0277.01 refers);
31.2 auction revenue generated from the competitive licensing process will accrue to the Crown, less any administrative costs of running the auction (CAB-24-MIN-0435 refers);
31.3 during the first few years of the scheme’s operation, licence fees should be set as a fixed percentage of a platform’s New Zealand-based Gross Gambling Revenue (GGR)2 with fee reviews every three to five years to avoid under- or over-recovery of the regulator’s costs (CAB-24-MIN-0435 refers).
I propose the regulations establish an Expressions of Interest fee and a General Licensing Levy
32. In line with Cabinet’s previous decisions and the feedback from targeted stakeholder engagement, I propose a cost recovery approach based on:
32.1 establishing a separate Expressions of Interest (EOI) fee paid by all operators wanting to participate in the competitive licensing process; and,
32.2 recovering the regulator’s remaining costs from a General Licensing Levy (apart from the cost of administering the auction process, which Cabinet has agreed should be recovered from revenue generated by the licence auction).
33. The non-refundable EOI fee would be payable by applicants at the time they submit their EOI. The ongoing General Licensing Levy would also be payable by licensed operators to the regulator with payment periods aligned with operators’ GST payment periods and dates.
34. This approach ensures the regulator’s costs for processing EOI are attributed to those parties generating them and benefiting from the ability to participate in the auction, while the regulator’s other costs will be met by the licensed operators that will benefit from holding
licences. It simplifies cost recovery in the system’s establishment phase and leaves open the option of introducing specific fees once the regulator has a better understanding of regulated party behaviour and its actual operating costs.
35. I also anticipate this approach will result in a more efficient EOI process by reducing the number of operators submitting an EOI who do not meet eligibility requirements.
To reduce the risk of under- or over-recovery, officials require more time to recommend actual fee and levy rates within proposed ceilings
36. To help ensure the EOI fee and General Licensing Levy rates, as far as practical, do not underor over-recover the regulator’s costs, the Department needs more time to complete work on the assessment of the regulator’s ongoing costs and their forecasts of GGR.
37. Therefore, I propose Cabinet delegate authority to me as the Minister of Internal Affairs to establish the final EOI fee and General Licensing Levy rates within the following fee ceilings:
37.1 EOI Fee up to NZ$19,000; and
37.2 General Licensing Levy rate up to 5% of each licensed operator’s GGR.
An initial review will enable rates to be adjusted at the time of licence renewal with guardrails used to trigger subsequent General Licensing Levy rate reviews
38. Given the uncertainty around forecast GGR, we also need to provide for the review of the proposed General Licensing Levy to moderate unintended under- or over-recovery. I propose, therefore, that the regulations provide for an initial levy rate review 24 months
after the first licence is issued, with any new levy rate coming into effect in conjunction with licence renewals 36 months after the first licences are approved
39. Provision should also be made for subsequent reviews 24 months after any levy rate change if cumulative levy revenue over a rolling 12-month period falls outside guardrails of +/-20% with any levy rate change coming into effect after 36 months. However, while a levy rate review may be triggered by revenue falling outside the guardrail, such a review may be superseded by, or subsumed within, any broader review of the primary or secondary legislation the government undertakes.
40. This approach should moderate the risk of under- or over-recovery while providing a reasonable degree of certainty to licensed operators about levy rate costs.
There should be penalties for late payment
41. I propose that the regulations make provision for a penalty for late payment of General Licensing Levy instalments. I suggest aligning the penalty with the provisions in the Gambling (Fees) Regulations 2015. The regulations would provide that if any instalment of an
operator’s levy is not paid within 20 working days of the due date, a penalty at the rate of 5% of that unpaid instalment would be applied. Moreover, a further penalty of 5% of the unpaid instalment and any previous penalties at the end of each subsequent month would be
charged, unless the Secretary considers imposing a further penalty would be unreasonable.

## Cost-of-living implications
46. The proposed regulations will not have a direct or immediate impact on the cost of living. Regulating online gambling may, however, help reduce financial losses to gamblers through strengthened harm minimisation and consumer protection measures.

## Financial implications
47. In line with Cabinet’s previous decisions and as provided for in the Bill and cost recovery regulations, the establishment and ongoing costs of operating the regulatory system are to be recovered from licensed online casino gambling operators.
48. The cost recovery regulations will enable the tagged contingency of $20 million provided to fund establishment costs to be repaid to the Crown within 10 years from draw down. This will make the capital injection fiscally neutral under the current Fiscal Management
Approach. The EOI fee and General Licensing Levy rate will also ensure the ongoing operating costs incurred by the Department for regulating the system will be fully funded.
49. While the Department is still developing the operating model and resulting costs for the regulator, it estimates the scheme’s ongoing costs will be in the range of annually (excluding capital charge and capital injection repayment).

## Population Implications
55. Online casino gambling has been found to have a larger impact on men, Māori, young people (aged 16 to 24 years) and Pacific women. However, participants in online casino gambling are spread across population groups and the effects of online casino gambling
flow to partners, children and families. The Office for Seniors and the Ministry for Disabled People – Whaikaha have noted that older New Zealanders and disabled New Zealanders are also groups that are at elevated risk of experiencing gambling harm. The establishment of the regulatory system is expected to protect consumers and prevent and minimise harm for people participating in online casino gambling.

## Human Rights
56. The Department has acknowledged that the inclusion of age restrictions in the advertising regulations may engage section 14 of the New Zealand Bill of Rights Act (BORA); every person has the right to seek, receive, and impart information and opinions of any kind in any
form.
57. I consider any encroachment on section 14 rights is justifiable. As already noted, children and young people in particular are susceptible to advertising and are vulnerable to gambling harm. The proposed age limits recognise this risk. Moreover, the regulations do not prevent those aged 18-25 from participating in online gambling.

## Appendix B: List of proposed regulations on advertising and marketing
The following requirements will apply to all licensed online casino operators that choose to advertise:
Permissible target audience and permissible forms of advertising
• Operators must not target advertisements at those under the age of 25 unless the operator can demonstrate that the advertisement can be precisely targeted at consumers over the age of 18 (for example, advertising at an R18 movie showing would be allowed);
• Wherever reasonably practicable, targeted age-gating must be utilised to ensure advertising is only shown to those over the age of 18;
• Advertising during, or 30 minutes before or after, a live broadcast of an event that would reasonably attract viewership of children is prohibited;
• Placing advertisements where more than 20% of the expected audience is under the age of 18 is prohibited;
• Placing advertisements where those under the age of 18 are reasonably likely to be exposed to frequent online casino gambling advertising is prohibited;
• Operators must exclude those who have self-excluded, or those identified by an operator as a problem gambler, from receiving direct and loyalty programme advertising;
• The only permitted forms of advertising are those stipulated in the regulations, and would be: broadcast, on-demand, published, outdoor, event, social media, digital product, interactive, direct marketing and search engine marketing;
Requirements for advertisements
• Advertising must be easily identifiable and labels must be obvious, clear, prominent and upfront and must be separate from other disclosures (e.g. hashtags);
• Interactive advertising (e.g. gamified advertisements, pop-ups) is only permitted on the operator’s platform;
• The use of a player’s name to customise an advertisement is prohibited;
• The use player metrics to customise advertisements that promote increased speed or intensity of play is prohibited;
• Advertising of jackpot prizes is prohibited;
Use of third parties
•  Affiliate marketing is prohibited
•  The use of paid endoresements is prohibited
• Operators are responsible for the actions of any other third party they contract with and must ensure they abide by the same regulations as the operator;
Advertising of bonuses and inducements
• Operators are permitted to offer bonuses and inducements to new and existing customers, but they must not be offered to customers that have self-excluded or customers showing strong signs of harm. This includes those identified as problem gamblers;
• Bonuses and inducements are not permitted to be used to disincentivise an existing customer closing their account or incentivise an individual to reopen their account;
• Permitted bonus or incentives cannot offer a benefit marketed as 'free' unless it actually is free;
• Bonuses and inducements can only be offered via direct marketing or on the operator’s platform. Players must be provided an opt-in process whereby they actively consent to receiving any direct marketing of inducements, bonuses and credits, and must be provided a
method to withdraw their consent at any time;
Restrictions on advertising at events (e.g. advertising at venues, or a booth at a conference)
• Advertising is only permitted at any event where attendees are all verified to be over the age of 18, access to the event is controlled and non-attendees are not exposed to any advertisement or advertising material;
• Event advertising is permitted but may not incentivise attendance e.g. through gifts, prizes, payment in kind, or other incentives;
• Event advertising must be overtly associated with the brand but must not be in conjunction with any other non-gambling activity or product. For example, operators would not be permitted to promote gambling in conjunction with drinking alcohol or advertise at nongambling event such as a trivia night or Rugby World Cup watch party;
Restrictions on direct marketing (e.g. emails, SMS, phone calls, push notifications)
• Operators must provide customers with options to opt-in to direct marketing on a per producttype and per channel basis. Customers must be able to select the number, frequency and type of notifications alongside channel usage. The options must cover all product types and channels provided by the operator and be set to opt-out by default. These options must be offered as part of the registration process and be able to be updated should customers change their preference;
• Operators are prohibited from sending marketing material to any player who has self-excluded or is subject to an operator-led exclusion. This includes customers who are yet to make a positive decision to return to gambling after their self-exclusion has expired;
• Direct marketing may only be sent to accountholders;
Restrictions on the content of advertisements and promotions
• The content of advertisements and promotions must not reasonably appeal to those under the age of 18. Examples of content that would be prohibited under this requirement include, but are not limited to:
o Depicting anyone who is, or seems to be under 18 years old unless the person is shown in an incidental role in a natural situation and where there is no implication they will engage in gambling activities (e.g. depicting a family dinner);
o Featuring anyone who is, or seems to be under 25 years old participating in gambling;
o Depicting content that uses or contains cartoon figures, puppets, animatronics, mascots or animation that would likely be appealing to children or young people;
o Depicting characters that are or are similar to 'cuddly’ or ‘cute’ animals, common fairy tales, like Little Red Riding hood, and Hansel and Gretel, characters popular with people under 18 years old (e.g. princesses, pirates, Bluey), and cultural characters like Santa Claus, the Tooth Fairy and the Easter Bunny;
o Content similar to video games and eSports popular with people under 18 years old
o Depicting toys, play scenes or playgrounds.
• Advertisements must not cause, condone, or encourage extended, continuous, excessive and impulsive play. Examples of content that would be prohibited under this requirement include, but are not limited to:
o Portraying gambling as a means of relieving or improving a person’s financial, professional or personal difficulties;
o Stating or implying a link between gambling and sexual or relationship success, enhanced attractiveness;
o Suggesting that gambling can enhance personal qualities, for example, that it can improve self-image or self-esteem, or is a way to gain control, superiority, recognition or admiration;
o Portraying, condoning or encouraging peer pressure;
o Portraying gambling as indispensable or as a priority in life;
o Suggesting gambling is a rite of passage;
o Linking gambling to toughness, resilience and recklessness;
o Condoning or featuring gambling in a working environment;
o Encouraging excessive participation (beyond a customer’s means);
o Portraying, condoning or encouraging gambling in combination with the consumption of alcohol;
o Promoting gambling as an alternative to employment, as a financial investment, or as a requirement for financial security;
o Encouraging plays as a means of recovering past gambling or other financial losses;
o Suggesting that gambling can provide an escape from personal or professional problems, e.g. loneliness or depression.
• Advertisements must not include the expressions “Win” or “$” unless these expressions specifically relate to a prize that has been determined or is payable, or to an estimate of a prize which can be won;
• Advertisements must be truthful and shall not mislead players or misrepresent products. Examples of content that would be prohibited under this requirement include, but are not limited to:
o Making claims related to winning or the prizes that can be won that are not based on fact, are unable to be proven or are exaggerated;
o Implying that the chances of winning increase the longer one plays or the more one spends;
o Suggesting that a player’s skill can influence the outcome of gambling activity in relation to gambling where a player’s skill cannot influence the outcome;
o Exaggerating the extent to which skill can influence the outcome of gambling activity in relation to gambling where a player’s skill can influence the outcome;
o Misrepresenting the level of financial risk;
o Creating a false sense of urgency such that consumers may be misled into thinking they must act quickly to participate or win;
• Advertisements must not play on people’s fears, beliefs or superstitions or use cultural symbols, names or imagery likely to cause serious or widespread offence;
• Advertisements must not include images or sounds suggestive of:
o Coins being inserted or dispensed from a gaming machine;
o Banknotes being inserted into or dispensed from a gaming machine or automated table game equipment;
o Tickets being printed or dispensed from a gaming machine or automated table game equipment; Poker chips; or
o Electronic Gaming Machine (slots) or jackpot winnings.
• Advertisements must not promote or otherwise imply a connection between gambling activity and use (individual/ family/communities) to which the profits of gambling providers may be put.
