
# Minimum Standards Consultation March 2026 New Zealand Online Casino Gambling

## ENGAGEMENT DRAFT - Not Government Policy

### Introduction
The Department of Internal Affairs (the Department) is developing minimum standards for Online Casino Gambling Technology (OCGT) as part of New Zealand’s online casino gambling regulatory system.
This is a targeted technical consultation for operators, independent testing laboratories and harm minimisation providers, that will be directly involved in meeting, assessing, or supporting compliance with the minimum standards for online casino gambling technology.
The purpose of this consultation document is to seek feedback on the proposed requirements to be included in minimum standards as well as the associated testing and monitoring requirements. We want to ensure the standards are clear, practical and effective.
To support this, we are particularly interested in feedback on:
• Compliance impacts – whether any requirements create challenges or an unnecessary burden.
• Benefit realisation – whether a particular requirement is particularly effective in minimising harm or providing consumer protection.
• Alternative approaches – whether there are other ways to meet the objective of a proposed requirement.
• Clarity and feasibility – where further explanation or refinement may be needed.
• Gaps or omissions – anything missing that should be included.
Your contributions are a vital part of the consultation process and we welcome all feedback. Submissions will be considered by the Secretary for Internal Affairs (the Secretary) when deciding what minimum standards are to be issued once the Online Casino Gambling Bill 2025 comes into force.

### Legislative and regulatory context
The Online Casino Gambling Bill 2025 (the Bill) was introduced to Parliament in June 2025.
The Bill establishes a licensing system for online casino gambling to support a safer, compliant, and regulated market. It is anticipated that the Bill will receive Royal Assent and come into force in May 2026.
Cabinet has agreed to the development of regulations to clearly establish licensed operators’ compliance requirements, support effective regulatory management, and enable operators to complete the licensing process.
The regulations currently being finalised fall under the following four categories, in accordance with Clauses 75-78 of the Bill:
• harm prevention and minimisation;
• consumer protection and record-keeping;
• advertising and marketing; and
• fees, levies, and charges for cost recovery.
The settings for these regulations were agreed by Cabinet following targeted consultation in 2025. The Cabinet Paper and Associated Materials provide detailed information on the decisions taken, and key points have been summarised in this document for ease of reference. These settings will not be re-considered as part of this consultation.

### Minimum standards, testing, and monitoring
Clause 80 of the Bill provides the Secretary with the power to issue minimum standards for online casino gambling technology (OCGT), relating to the design, manufacture, performance, and functions of OCGT, as well as information displayed or generated by OCGT.
The Bill also enables the Secretary to specify testing and monitoring requirements to ensure that operators comply with those standards.

### Scope and approach of this consultation
This consultation paper sets out the Secretary’s proposals for:
• minimum standards for OCGT; and
• testing and monitoring requirements to demonstrate compliance with those standards.
Each section is followed by consultation questions relating directly to the content covered.
The proposals are intended to support the objectives of the Bill, including establishing a licensing regime, preventing and minimising gambling harm, and protecting consumers.
The Department intends to be a responsive, risk‑based regulator, and the proposed requirements are designed to be proportionate to the risks posed by online casino gambling. Where possible, requirements align with international standards to support practical implementation by operators and to ensure the framework is informed by established best practice and evidence of effectiveness observed overseas. We are particularly interested in hearing about your experience in implementing or testing against similar requirements in other jurisdictions.

### Harm Minimisation and Consumer Protection
The information in the grey boxes reflects the Legislative or Regulatory settings that have been agreed by Cabinet. This material is provided for information only and is out of scope for this consultation.
Identity Verification:
Regulatory setting: Operators must collect and verify a customer’s identity at the account creation stage before the customer is permitted to make a deposit. As part of this process, operators must determine whether the customer has self-excluded from gambling. An acceptable identity verification system must confirm the customer’s full name, date of birth, and self-exclusion status. Operators must obtain and verify this information before allowing any deposit activity. Operators must also verify that the person using an account is the actual account holder.
Clause 38 of the Online Casino Gambling Bill 2025 (“the Bill”) requires an operator to take all reasonable steps to ensure that a person who participates in online casino gambling on the operator’s platform is at least 18 years old.
Proposed minimum standards
The Secretary proposes that the minimum standards will include requirements that:
1. All customer accounts must be uniquely identifiable, and customers must authenticate via at least a username and password each time they login.
2.The gambling platform must maintain an auditable trail of events that are logged and available, relating to account creation and activation, account deactivation and account changes.
3. The gambling platform must give customers the option to use multi-factor authentication to govern sensitive activities including but not limited to login, adding a new deposit or withdrawal method or resetting passwords.
4. There must be a customer activity time-out that automatically logs the customer out or ends the customer’s session after a specified period of inactivity.
These settings are similar to 3.08, 3.10, 3.12 and 4.30 of the Alcohol and Gaming Commission of Ontario (AGCO) Registrar’s Standards for Internet Gaming.

### Consumer Location
Legislative setting:
Clause 4(3) of the Bill refers to the Act as only applying to persons to the extent that gambling is conducted in a manner that enables a person who is in New Zealand to participate in the gambling.
Clause 9(1) allows a person to conduct online casino gambling in a manner that enables a person who is in New Zealand to participate in the gambling if the person conducts the online casino gambling in accordance with a licence.
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that the minimum standards will include requirements that:
1.The gambling platform shall only be available to a person who is in New Zealand.
2.The gambling platform must have mechanisms to detect and dynamically monitor the location of a consumer attempting to access the gambling platform and to block unverified attempts to access the platform. Consumer location checks after the initial location check shall occur at reasonable intervals determined by the operator that minimise the risk of play outside of New Zealand.
3.The gambling platform must have mechanisms to detect software, programs, virtualization and other programs capable of circumventing customer location detection.
These settings are similar to 3.02 of the AGCO Registrar’s Standards for Internet Gaming.

### Informed decision making
Regulatory setting:
Before placing a bet, customers must be given enough information to make informed decisions. Odds of winning and losing must be described accurately. Operators must provide information and services in a form, language, and manner that customers can understand.
Information about a customer’s current account balance and playing history must be easy to find on the platform. Where connection speed or system capability may affect gameplay, this must be clearly communicated to players.
Any limits or restrictions on withdrawing winnings must be clearly explained and must not extend beyond the specific game being played.
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that the minimum standards will include requirements that:
1.Safer gambling materials and information about obtaining help must be clearly visible and easy to access on the platform. Safer gaming material should include information about:
a.How games work and about common misconceptions;
b.Lower risk gambling behaviours including how responsible gambling tools work;
c.Gambling harms; and
d.The variety of support services available to customers, including information and support services available to customers that may provide specialised information (e.g., self-assessment, and play management tools).
2.All information related to safer gambling available on the gambling platform must be regularly and periodically reviewed and updated to ensure that it is accurate, up to date and in line with industry good practice.
3.All gaming sessions must clearly display a customer’s account balance and net position since the session started, in NZ Dollars (NZD).
4.The Operator must give the customer access to the following information via the gambling platform:
a.Deposit and withdrawal history, and current balance;
b.Method and source of funds used for transactions;
c.Date and time of all previous logins;
d.Gaming event and transaction history (game session outcomes and game transactions) including the date and time of past and current bets, and the date and time at which past bets were settled, and information about current bets;
e.Total monies wagered for session and/or period of time;
f.Total monies won or lost for session and/or period of time; and
g.Account balance at start and end of session.
5.All customer account transactions shall be uniquely identifiable and traceable to a unique individual customer account.
6. The gambling platform must be able to:
a. Inform customers that the speed of connection or processor may have, or appear to have, an effect on the game;
b.Recover from failures that cause interruptions to the game in a timely fashion;
c.Where appropriate, void bets;
d.Retain sufficient information to be able to restore events to their pre-failure state, if possible;
e.Pay customers the amount won up to that point, or return bets to customers where a game cannot be continued after a service interruption, whichever is the better outcome for the customer.
These settings are similar to 2.08, 2.21, 3.15 and 4.21 of the AGCO Registrar’s Standards for Internet Gaming and RTS 2, RTS4, and RTS10 of the UK Gambling Commission’s Remote Gambling and Software Technical Standards.

### Game Rules
Regulatory setting:
The rules of a game and an explanation of how it works must be easy to access on the platform. It must also be clear who or what the customer is playing against.
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that the minimum standards will include requirements that:
1.Comprehensive and accurate information explaining the terms governing play must be clearly visible and easy to access on the platform. This information may be available on game selection pages or menus, or within individual games, before a customer places a bet or wager. It may be provided through supports such as “game rules,” “help,” or “how to play” pages, which must be prominently positioned so customers can easily locate them.
2.The theoretical return of the game must be accurately presented as a return to player (RTP) percentage.
3.Where relevant, as the game or event progresses, information that may reasonably be expected to enable the customer to understand the current state must be displayed.
These settings are similar to 4.06 of the AGCO Registrar’s Standards for Internet Gaming and RTS3 of the UK Gambling Commission’s Remote Gambling and Software Technical Standards.

### Game Design and Features
Regulatory setting:
Game designs and user interface features must be clear and not mislead and must be designed to prevent extended, continuous and impulsive play, including by prohibiting the use of auto-play.
Game designs and features must not give players the false impression that speed of play or player skill affects the outcome when it does not. Games must not display goals or achievements that cannot be reached.
Customers will be limited to playing one online slot game on a platform at any one time
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that the minimum standards will include requirements that:
1.Game designs and operations must match game rules, and that results are settled in accordance with the rules.
2. A losing bet outcome must display only the game’s outcomes as per the stated rules.
3.Where a paid game has significantly different odds/RTP to an equivalent free-to-play game on the operator’s platform, this must be clearly communicated to the player before they make a wager.
4.Game play must only be initiated after the customer has placed a wager and activated play. No customer shall be forced into game play by selecting the game for review or reviewing information about how the game is played or how bets are made.
5.The gambling platform must ensure a customer commits to each game individually, releasing and then depressing the 'start button’ or taking equivalent action. Continued contact with a button, key or screen must not initiate a new game or game cycle.
6.There must be a minimum of 2.5 seconds between the start of a game and the point at which the next game cycle can begin. To commence a game cycle, the customer must release and then press the start button or take an equivalent action. It must never be possible for a game to automatically begin a new cycle without this deliberate player action.
A game cycle begins when the customer presses the start button or takes an equivalent action to initiate the game. The cycle ends when all money or monies worth staked or won during that game has been lost, delivered to the customer, or made available for collection, and the start button or equivalent becomes available for initiating the next game.
7.The gambling platform must not permit a customer to reduce the time until a result is presented (e.g. features such as turbo, quick spin and slam stop are not permitted).
8.The gambling platform must ensure customers have a means to always track the passage of time.
9.If the platform uses full screen client applications that obscure the time, the platform must always display the time of day in New Zealand, or the elapsed time since a game session was started.
10.Combining multiple slots titles in a way which facilitates simultaneous play will not be permitted.
11.Where the game requires a pre-determined pattern (for example, hidden prizes on a map), the locations of the winning spots shall not change during play, except as provided for in the terms governing play.
These settings are similar to 2.15-2.22 of the AGCO Registrar’s Standards for Internet Gaming and RTS5, RTS 6, RTS 8, RTS 13 and RTS 14 and of the UK Gambling Commission’s Remote Gambling and Software Technical Standards.

### Random Number Generator (RNG)
Legislative Setting:
Clause 5 of the Bill defines Online Casino Gambling as including gambling at a distance by interaction through a communication device, including using the communication device to participate in gambling that relies on a random number generator or other wholly or partly chance-based outcome generation.
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that minimum standards will include requirements that:
1.Random number generation and game results must be ‘acceptably random’. Acceptably random here means that it is possible to demonstrate to a high degree of confidence that the output of the RNG, game or computer simulated sporting event are random through, for example, statistical analysis using generally accepted tests and methods of analysis. Adaptive behaviour (that is, a compensated game) is not permitted.
2.The output from the RNG must be uniformly distributed over the entire output range. Game and computer simulated sporting event outcomes are distributed in accordance with the expected or theoretical probabilities.
3.The output of the RNG, game or computer simulated sporting event must be unpredictable. For a software RNG it should be computationally infeasible to predict what the next number will be without complete knowledge of the algorithm and seed value.
4.Random number generation must not reproduce the same output stream (cycle), and two instances of a RNG must not produce the same stream as each other (synchronise).
5. Any forms of seeding and re-seeding used do not introduce predictability.
These settings are similar to 4.26 of the AGCO Registrar’s Standards for Internet Gaming and RTS 7 of the UK Gambling Commission’s Remote Gambling and Software Technical Standards.

### Breaks in play and pop-up alerts (or reality checks)
Regulatory setting:
Operators must offer players the ability to set voluntary breaks in play and voluntary personalised popup alerts. Players must be able to choose how often these breaks and alerts occur. Operators must offer a range of time intervals that players can select from, with one hour being presented as the default option for pop-up alerts and intervals similar to those required in Germany and Norway for breaks in play.
When a popup alert appears, gameplay must pause or be suspended until the player acknowledges it. The alert may appear at the end of a game, or at the end of a round for multistage games such as blackjack. However, a player cannot be allowed to commit further funds to a new game until they have acknowledged the alert. At a minimum, each popup alert must show the player’s session time and the total amount lost during that session.
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that the minimum standards will include requirements that:
1.The ability to set voluntary breaks in play and personalised pop-up alerts is easily available on the platform at any time after the customer has opened an account.
2.The gambling platform must allow a customer to determine how often an in game break in play occurs (for example, setting a break after every hour of play) as well as the duration of the break.
The settings relating to pop-up alerts are similar to RTS 13 of the UK Gambling Commission’s Remote Gambling and Software Technical Standards.

### Identifying and assisting individuals who may be experiencing harm
Regulatory setting:
Operators must have policies and procedures in place to monitor, identify, and assist customers who may be experiencing gambling harm. The information collected for this purpose will be determined by the Secretary.
Operators must ensure their staff are trained and equipped to understand gambling harm, recognise signs of problematic behaviour, and provide appropriate assistance.
Operators must actively encourage customers to use the safer gambling tools and exclusion options available to them. They must also clearly signpost customers to external support services.
When concerning behaviour is identified, the operator must communicate with the customer and provide a clear explanation of the behaviours that triggered the concern.
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that minimum standards will include requirements that:
1.The operator’s platform must be capable of monitoring play and identifying customers who may be experiencing gambling harm.
2.The contact information for external support services which assist people who may be experiencing harm from gambling must be easily available on the gambling platform at any time after the customer has opened an account.
3.Live customer support shall be made available on the gambling platform at all times.
These settings are similar to 2.11 of the AGCO Registrar’s Standards for Internet Gaming.

### Self-exclusion
Regulatory setting:
Operators must offer customers the ability to self-exclude, and the self-exclusion process must be easy to find, easy to understand, and simple to complete. They must offer self-exclusion on a per-platform or per-operator basis and must also exclude individuals they identify as problem gamblers.
When a customer initiates a self-exclusion, the operator must immediately exclude the customer without undue delay. When the operator identifies a customer as a problem gambler, they must exclude that customer from all branded online casino platforms they control, also without undue delay.
When a customer is excluded, whether through self-exclusion or operator-led exclusion, the operator must cease all communication with that customer. This includes refraining from initiating contact at the end of a self-exclusion period unless the customer has already taken active steps to return to gambling.
Operators must also participate in a centralised self-exclusion register if an appropriate register is made available.
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that minimum standards will include requirements that:
1.Operators’ self-exclusion processes must be well promoted and easily available on the gambling platform at any time after the customer has opened an account.
2.The gambling platform must maintain a register of those excluded with appropriate records, including name, and any membership or account details that may be held by the operator.
3.The gambling platform must be able to identify any person who is excluded from gambling, and must prevent that person from gambling on the platform.
These settings are similar to 2.14 of the AGCO Registrar’s Standards for Internet Gaming.

### Preventing player collusion and cheating in peer-to-peer casino games
Regulatory setting:
Operators must prevent collusion, cheating, and the use of software programs designed to participate in gameplay automatically. Operators must have policies and procedures in place to detect and prevent these behaviours, and these must be available to players on the platform and on request.
Players must be provided with clear information about how to report suspected collusion, cheating, or automated gambling.
The platform must offer an easy way for players to submit these reports. All complaints relating to cheating, collusion, or automated gambling must be investigated by the operator.
Proposed minimum standards:
The Secretary proposes that minimum standards ensure operators prevent collusion and cheating including the use of auto-play by specifying that:
1.Gambling platforms must be capable of identifying where players are colluding, cheating or participating in automated gambling (where a player automatically participate in game play (referred to as a bot)).
2.Gambling systems must retain a record of relevant activities to facilitate investigation, and be capable of suspending or disabling player accounts or player sessions.

### Testing Requirements
The testing and monitoring requirements set out when and how an operator must monitor and test any online casino gambling technology.

### Summary
In order to demonstrate compliance with the Regulations and Minimum Standards, the Secretary is proposing testing and monitoring requirements on operators, similar to those in the UK Testing Strategy for Compliance with Remote Gambling and Software Technical Standards. In summary the Secretary proposes that operators will be:
• Required to undertake pre‑release testing of new games, RNGs, and major platform integrations by an approved ITL. Operators must also complete re‑testing by a specified ITL for major updates to existing games that affect the RNG, the game rules, or how those rules are applied in the game logic.
• Permitted to rely on internal assessment for minor updates that do not impact the RNG, game rules, or how game rules are applied in the game logic, provided they adhere to good practice guidelines.
• Required to have an annual games testing and platform certification audit conducted by a specified ITL.
• Required to have an annual third-party information, security and access audit conducted by a specified ITL or by any other person specified by the Secretary.
• Required to monitor real-time game performance.
• Required to perform external quarterly vulnerability scans and annual penetration testing of their platform.
To allow operators sufficient time to undertake the external testing requirements, the Secretary is proposing that operators will not be required to have completed external testing, under the proposed testing and monitoring requirements, during the first six months of being licensed. This would apply where operators can demonstrate that their OCGT has been tested to the minimum standards of an equivalent jurisdiction.

### Proposals
Testing by a specified independent test laboratory
The Secretary is proposing that pre-release testing is required by a specified Independent Test Laboratory (ITL) for all new games, RNGs and major platform integrations.
Some re-testing by a specified ITL will also be required for updates to existing games that impact the RNG, game rules, or how game rules are applied in the game-logic (major updates).
Where external testing is required by a specified ITL an operator must ensure they receive, and maintain ITL test reports which must include:
• details of the testing performed,
• software versions and digital signatures,
• dates of testing, and
• Designed RTP for games and for RNGs any limitations on its use (e.g., maximum acceptable degrees of freedom).

### Internal testing by an operator
The Secretary is proposing that where an update does not impact the RNG, game rules, or how game rules are applied in the game-logic (minor update) the operator’s internal assessment as to whether the update complies with the Regulations and Minimum Standards will be sufficient.
To be permitted to carry out their own testing of online casino gambling technology operators will be required to adhere to good practice guidelines in development, and testing release of online casino technology as set out below:
Development:
• Source Code Security: Source code must be maintained in a secure, access-controlled environment with an auditable log of all access to prevent unauthorised manipulation and maintain code integrity.
• Version Control: Older versions of source code, including retirement dates, must be retained to ensure a full audit trail and rollback capability in case of system failure or compliance investigation.
• Access Control: Access for developers must be strictly controlled based on the principle of minimum privilege, granting only the access necessary for the specific role.
• Documentation: Source code must be accompanied by comprehensive technical documentation suitable for independent review and future maintenance.
• Code Quality: Source files need sufficient commenting, legibility, and structure to permit static code analysis and confident functional review by external parties.
• Segregation of Duties: Write access to the core platform source code must be restricted from personnel focused only on game-specific development to prevent unauthorised modification of critical systems.
• Peer Review: Changes to critical modules must be peer-reviewed by suitably skilled, independent developers to ensure alignment with documentation and quality standards before being merged.

### Testing:
• Environment Separation: Development, testing, and production environments must be logically and physically segregated to prevent development bugs or unauthorised code from accidentally entering the live system.
• Personnel Separation: Testing should be performed by staff independent of those who developed the product to ensure impartial and objective assessment of the product's functionality and compliance.

### Release process:
• Change Management Plan: All releases must follow a documented process to ensure adequate internal testing for minor changes and ITL testing for major changes.
• RNG/Game Change: Accompanying any RNG/game change, the change documentation must record:

▪delivery channel(s)
▪description of change

▪relevant manager / change board authorisation for change
▪other particulars as required by the licence holder’s internal change management requirements.

### Annual games testing and platform certification audit
To ensure operators are correctly categorising updates (that is, major or minor) and following defined procedures for the development, testing, release, and RTP monitoring of games, the Secretary is proposing that an annual game testing and platform certification audit must be undertaken by a specified ITL. To support this audit, operators will be required to maintain, and make available to the Secretary, a games register that details the games and RNGs they operate, along with the testing that has been performed.
The annual game testing and platform certification audit must:
• assess OCGT development, testing and release processes against the good practice guidelines;
• assess RTP monitoring processes in accordance with RTP monitoring guidelines.
• review games being offered as contained in the games register and confirm that adequate testing was conducted through a sample of changes that were tested internally and externally;
• certify a platform is compliant, including assessing: customer onboarding processes, reporting of game and account history, and the availability of required responsible gambling tools such as limits and pop-up alerts; and
• audit live dealer operations to ensure the fairness and integrity of those operations. Audits of live dealer operations conducted for other equivalent jurisdictions will be accepted to minimis unnecessary duplication.
Annual games testing and platform certification audit reports will be required to be submitted to the Secretary. The final audit report must contain management responses where relevant and remediation plans to any negative findings.

### Ongoing Monitoring
Despite robust development and testing processes, games and platforms may still contain faults and vulnerabilities. To mitigate this risk operators will be required to monitor the performance of their games and platforms to detect potential issues. The objective is to ensure timely detection and recovery that maximises customer fairness. The Secretary proposes that:
1. Operators will be required to monitor the real-time performance of the games they provide, including through third parties, to ensure they are operating as intended. This monitoring will assess the actual Return to Player (RTP) against the game designs and published rules to ensure they are consistent.
2. Any major game or platform faults (i.e. impacting on compliance with the regulatory regime) or material unplanned platform outages need to be documented and reported to the regulator.
3. To ensure security, operators will be required to perform external quarterly vulnerability scans and annual penetration testing of their platform and retain evidence of how they responded to identified issues.
Where incidents are identified with OCGT there is an expectation this be reported within five working days. Reportable incidents would include faults or outages with OCGT that do, or could impact compliance with the law, regulations and minimum standards, and include:
• Confirmed faults with a game’s RTP or RNG;
• Unplanned outages that affect a material amount of customers; and
• Material information security breaches to the platform.
