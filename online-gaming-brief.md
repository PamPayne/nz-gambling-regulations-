
# New Zealand Online Gambling Briefing – 23 January 2026

## Race and Sports Betting – TAB New Zealand’s New Online Monopoly
• From 28 June 2025, it is unlawful for anyone apart from TAB New Zealand to offer online race and sports betting to persons located in New Zealand. The prohibition is extraterritorial (it applies regardless of where the online provider is resident or incorporated).
• From 28 June 2025, it is unlawful for New Zealanders to place race and sports bets with anyone apart from TAB New Zealand.
• The prohibition includes race and sports betting events that take place both within and outside New Zealand.
• The prohibition is set out in section 74AAA of the Racing Industry Act and the definition of “remote interactive gambling” in section 4 of the Gambling Act.

## Online Casino Gambling - New Zealand’s Proposed Online Gambling Licensing System
The Licensing System and Timing
• Legislation for a licensing system for online casinos (the Online Casino Gambling Bill) was introduced to the New Zealand Parliament on 30 June 2025. The expected timeframe is as follows:
o Select Committee consultation process – completed.
o Bill enacted – January/February 2026.
o Commence of the new legislation – 1 May 2026 – New stronger advertising prohibitions commence. The new prohibitions include significantly increased penalties for breaches.
o Regulations published – sector engagement commences to assist with understanding the new obligations – June/July 2026.
o Expressions of interest for a licence open – July 2026.
o Licence auction – September 2026.
o Licence application processing begins – October 2026.
o New online casino gambling licences issued – from 1 December 2026. 
o Grey market ends - only providers who have applied for a licence may conduct online casino gambling – 1 December 2026.
•If a provider has applied for a licence before 1 December 2026, the prohibition on conducting online casino gambling does not apply until either the licence application is determined or 1 June 2027, whichever occurs first. The prohibition on advertising online gambling will, however, apply.
•From 1 December 2026, only providers who have either applied for or obtained a licence may offer online casino gambling to persons located in New Zealand. This provision will be extra-territorial (it will apply regardless of whether the provider is based in or outside 
New Zealand).

## Taxes, Fees, Levies, and Community Return
• A 12% online casino duty is payable. This will increase to 16% from 1 January 2027. The additional 4% will be ringfenced and used for community returns. The 4% will be distributed by the Lottery Grants Board. The amount of the duty/community return will be reviewed in 
December 2028. The review will include an assessment of the impact of online casino gambling on other forms of gambling and their community returns. A further, full review of the online casino gambling Regulatory system will be undertaken in December 2029.
• A 15% goods and services tax (GST) is payable.
• A 1.24% problem gambling levy is payable from 1 December 2026.
• An expression of interest fee of up to $19,000.00 is payable (the exact amount will be set by the Minister of Internal Affairs).
• A general licensing levy of up to 5% of the platform's New Zealand-based Gross Gambling Revenue will be payable (the exact amount will be set by the Minister of Internal Affairs). Gross Gambling Revenue is the total amount of money wagered by players minus the 
winnings paid out, representing the revenue a gaming provider earns before deducting expenses and taxes. The amount is GST inclusive. The levy will be payable in periods aligned with the provider’s GST periods and dates (typically monthly or bi-monthly). The fee will be reviewed 24 months after the first licence is issued and any adjustment will take place 36 months after the first licence is approved as part of the licence renewal process.

## No New Zealand Presence
• It will not be necessary for licensed providers to have a physical presence in New Zealand. Providers must have a New Zealand address for service. The lack of a New Zealand presence may, however, be taken into account when assessing the applicant’s ability to hold a 
licence.

## Number of Licences and Licence Term
•  There will be 15 licences available, allocated by auction.
• One licence will be required per platform/brand/website. The licence limit will apply to platforms, rather than providers.
• Any single provider can hold no more than three of the 15 total licences. A person cannot not have a significant influence over more than three licences. “Significant influence” is defined as having voting power, directly or indirectly, of 20% or more.
• The licences will be for a three-year period with one right of renewal of up to five years (a licence may only be renewed once). Renewals will not be granted automatically. The provider must have met its licence conditions and have a history of compliance with New 
Zealand law.
• Licences must be used within 90 days of being issued. The licenced gambling platform must be operating and available at least 270 days in every 12-month period. The Regulator will have the power to suspend or cancel vacant licences and issue replacement licences. This will prevent providers buying licences to stop their competitors from accessing the market.
• Vacant licences will be reissued via a competitive process determined by the Regulator.
• There will be a public register of licensed brands.

## Permitted Games
• Providers will be able to offer “online casino gambling”, which is defined as:
  o Gambling that relies on a random number generator or other wholly or partly chancebased outcome such as slot machine games, lotteries, bingo, roulette, craps, and wheel of fortune.
  o Casino table gambling such as blackjack, poker, or baccarat.
  o Betting on a computer-simulated sporting event; i.e., a sport, game, competition, race, or other similar event that is simulated by a computer and does not involve human or animal competitors.
• Activities that are not considered “online casino gambling” include: 
  o Betting on real-life racing or sporting events as these are not considered casino games.
  o Betting on novelty events (e.g., bird of the year, gender of a royal baby). A novelty event is defined as “any cultural, social, political, environmental, economic, or other similar event, whether it takes place in or outside New Zealand.” 
  o Online lotteries run by Class 3 incorporated societies (e.g., Heart Foundation and Coastguard) for fundraising purposes as these have a specific purpose to return all profits to community organisations.
  o Online casino gambling that is substantially similar to a protected New Zealand lottery.
  o Anything excluded as online casino gambling in regulations (the definition can be redefined to take account of future forms of casino gambling, if the need arises).
  o Activities where money is not gambled or won. For example, online games like Solitaire, and online video games like Minecraft with loot boxes do not fall within the definition.
• The Regulator cannot grant a licence to conduct a gambling product that is substantially similar to, and is likely to be in competition with, Lotto, Powerball, and/or Strike (products offered by the New Zealand Lotteries Commission).
• Synthetic lotteries are not permitted (a synthetic lottery is where players bet on the outcome of lottery draw with a third party).

## Three Stage Licence Allocation Process
• Licences will be allocated through a three-stage process: expressions of interest, an auction by persons invited by the Regulator, and a full licence application by the successful bidders.
• Stage 1 – Expressions of Interest
o The Regulator will invite expressions of interest for a licence by giving public notice. The notice will specify the minimum amount of capital the provider must have access to in order to conduct online casino gambling. 
o Expressions of interest must be submitted to the Regulator within 20 working days of public notice being given.
o The expression of interest must include:
    The full name and contact details of the provider’s chief executive, chief financial officer, chief operating officer, and any other person who has a significant level of influence over the decision making, management, or finances of the provider. 
    Information regarding the above persons’ suitability, including:
      • Any investigations, breaches, or convictions in the last seven years relating to gambling, consumer protection, or anti-money laundering legislation. 
      • Any prior bankruptcy, liquidation, or receivership.
      • Any prior prohibition on being a director or promotor or being involved in the management of a company.
      • Any prior breaches of an Advertising Standards Authority requirement or standard.
    Information about the other gambling licences the provider or its key person have held or been involved with. 
    Full disclosure of the provider’s ownership structure (including details about the jurisdiction in which each person who is part of the ownership structure is resident, incorporated, or otherwise has or is treated as having a presence). 
    Information about any contribution that the provider makes or intends to make for community purposes in New Zealand (whether an applicant has a presence in New Zealand and whether the applicant makes contributions for community purposes in New Zealand will be factors considered when assessing whether the applicant is suitable to hold a licence). 
    Information about any significant influence the provider has over an existing licence.
    Details of the intended gambling platform.
   Details of the intended branding. 
   Whether the provider has had any prior gambling licence suspended or cancelled or refused to be renewed.
    Information regarding the source and amount of capital available. 
o The expression of interest will only be accepted if the minimum capital requirement is met and the provider and its key persons have not been convicted of a crime involving dishonesty in the last seven years.
o A fixed, non-refundable expression of interest fee is payable of up to $19,000.00 (the exact amount will be determined by the Minister of Internal Affairs).
• Stage 2 – The Auction
o If the expression of interest is accepted, the provider will be invited to participate in the auction process.
o The Regulator will provide a minimum of 10 working days’ notice of when the auction will start and end. 
o The Regulator expects the auction cost to be a “medium cost” for licenced providers, “likely in the millions; however, low in proportion to likely profit.”
• Stage 3 – Application for a Licence
o Successful bidders may then formally apply for a licence. The auction amount must be paid prior to the licence application being submitted. The licence application must be lodged within 20 working days, or a longer period approved by the Regulator.
o If a licence application is not lodged within the required timeframe, the auction fee is refunded, less the Regulator’s responsible costs. If the licence application is declined, the auction fee is refunded, less the Regulator's responsible costs.
o The licence application must include:
  The provider’s advertising and marketing strategy. This must include:
   • The provider’s overall marketing objectives and intentions.
   • The provider’s proposed marketing methods and techniques, including branding (slogans and logos).
   • The provider’s strategy with regard to the frequency, volume, and type of advertisements that will be made; the channels and media through which advertisements will be made; the classes of consumers at which the advertisements will be targeted; and the use of any endorsements, sponsorships, or similar third-party arrangements. 
   • Any market research or data the provider has relied on to support its advertising and marketing decisions. 
   • How the provider intends to comply with the advertising regulations. 
   • The provider's consumer protection strategy. This must include the provider’s strategies, plans, policies, systems, and processes to verify the age and identity of consumers, ensure that the interests of consumers are protected, ensure that consumers are treated fairly, and ensure compliance with the online casino regulations. 
   • The provider’s harm prevention and minimisation strategy. This must include the provider’s strategies, plans, policies, systems, and processes to prevent and minimise harm from online casino gambling and to ensure compliance with the online casino gambling regulations. 
   • The provider’s compliance strategy. This must include the provider's strategies, plans, policies, systems and processes to comply with the Online Casino Gambling Act, the Gambling Act, the Anti-Money Laundering and Countering Financing of Terrorism Act, the Privacy Act, and the online casino gambling regulations.
   • The provider’s business plan including the provider’s goals and objectives, the provider’s management and operations structure, and the provider’s financial viability and projections. 
   • The provider’s physical address for service in New Zealand.
   • Details about the gambling platform, branding, and user interface that the provider proposes to use to conduct online casino gambling

## Advertising and Marketing Provisions in the Bill
• Licensed gambling providers will be allowed to advertise, but with strict limits. The rules around advertising will be set by regulations.
• Administrative communications between the provider and a player are excluded from the advertising definition and restrictions.
• Promotional material shown as a result of a physical sporting event that is held outside of New Zealand is excluded from the advertising definition and restrictions.
• The gambling platform, and any advertisement of the platform, must display a registration icon. During advertisements that include audio, a registration audio statement must be played, such as “Licensed under the Online Casino Gambling Act”.

## Proposed Regulations on Advertising and Marketing
Permissible Target Audience and Permissible Forms of Advertising
• Providers must not target advertisements at those under the age of 25 unless the provider can demonstrate that the advertisement can be precisely targeted at consumers over the age of 18 (for example, advertising at an R18 movie showing would be allowed).
• Wherever reasonably practicable, targeted age-gating must be used to ensure advertising is only shown to those over the age of 18.
• Advertising during, or 30 minutes before or after, a live broadcast of an event that would reasonably attract viewership of children is prohibited.
• Placing advertisements where more than 20% of the expected audience is under the age of 18 is prohibited.
• Placing advertisements where those under the age of 18 are reasonably likely to be exposed to frequent online casino gambling advertising is prohibited.
• Providers must exclude those who have self-excluded, or those identified by a provider as a problem gambler, from receiving direct and loyalty programme advertising.
• The only permitted forms of advertising are those stipulated in the regulations, namely:
o Broadcast – covers live broadcasts (e.g., TV and radio), live-streams and live-updates.
o On-demand – covers on-demand broadcasts and streaming (e.g. podcasts, streaming platforms, cinemas).
o Published – material published in print (e.g., newspapers, pamphlets), as well as digital publications and websites (e.g., news websites, eBooks). This includes advertising on a website hosted or controlled by the provider; e.g., a branded online casino gambling platform.
o Outdoor advertising – refers to mediums traditionally located in public places (e.g.,billboards, digital displays). 
o Events – refers to in-person and virtual events that can be hosted or attended by providers (e.g., private launch party, booth at a gambling conference).
o Social media – online platforms for users to connect, engage, and share content.
o Digital product advertising – advertisements in digital products (e.g., apps, video games, online games, programmes). This includes digital products hosted or controlled by the provider.
o Direct marketing – communications directed to a specific individual or group through any medium (e.g., SMS, email, push notifications, phone calls, mail).
o Search engine marketing – advertising undertaken to increase a website’s availability on search engines such as Google.
Requirements for Advertisements
• Advertising must be easily identifiable, and labels must be obvious, clear, prominent and upfront and must be separate from other disclosures (e.g., hashtags).
• Interactive advertising (e.g., gamified advertisements, pop-ups) is only permitted on the provider’s platform.
• The use of a player’s name to customise an advertisement is prohibited.
• The use of player metrics to customise advertisements that promote increased speed or intensity of play is prohibited.
• Advertising of jackpot prizes is prohibited.
Use of Third Parties
• Affiliate marketing is prohibited
• The use of social media influencers is prohibited.
• The use of paid endorsements is prohibited.
• Providers are responsible for the actions of any third party they contract with and must ensure the third party abides by the regulations.
Advertising of Bonuses and Inducements
• Providers are permitted to offer bonuses and inducements to new and existing players, but they must not be offered to players that have self-excluded or players showing strong signs of harm. This includes those identified as problem gamblers.
• Bonuses and inducements are not permitted to be used to disincentivise an existing player from closing their account or to incentivise an individual to reopen their account.
• Permitted bonus or incentives cannot offer a benefit marketed as 'free' unless it actually is free.
• Bonuses and inducements can only be offered via direct marketing or on the provider’s platform. Players must be provided with an opt-in process whereby they actively consent to receiving any direct marketing of inducements, bonuses and credits, and must be provided with a method to withdraw their consent at any time.
• The value of a bonus or inducement cannot exceed $100 or 200% of the value of the original deposit or bet, whichever is lesser in value.
• Bonuses and inducements must be offered in plain language, must not cause or encourage excessive, impulsive or high-risk play, and cannot be used to deter a player from withdrawing winnings or self-excluding themselves from gambling.
Restrictions on Advertising at Events (e.g., Advertising at Venues, or A Booth at a Conference)
• Advertising is only permitted at an event where all the attendees are verified to be over the 
age of 18, access to the event is controlled, and non-attendees are not exposed to any 
advertisement or advertising material.
• Attendance at the event cannot be incentivised; e.g., through gifts, prizes, payment in kind, or other incentives.
• Event advertising must be overtly associated with the brand and must not be in conjunction with any other non-gambling activity or product. For example, providers would not be permitted to promote gambling in conjunction with drinking alcohol, or to advertise at 
nongambling events such as a trivia night or a Rugby World Cup watch party.
Restrictions on Direct Marketing (e.g., Emails, SMS, Phone Calls, Push Notifications)
• Providers must provide players with options to opt-in to direct marketing on a per product type and per channel basis. Players must be able to select the number, frequency and type of notifications alongside channel usage. The options must cover all product types and 
channels offered by the provider and be set to opt-out by default. These options must be offered as part of the registration process and be able to be updated should players change their preferences.
• Providers are prohibited from sending marketing material to any player who has selfexcluded or is subject to a provider-led exclusion. This includes players who are yet to make a positive decision to return to gambling after their self-exclusion has expired.
• Direct marketing may only be sent to account holders.
Restrictions on The Content of Advertisements and Promotions
• The content of advertisements and promotions must not reasonably appeal to those under the age of 18. Examples of content that would be prohibited under this requirement include, but are not limited to:
o Depicting anyone who is, or seems to be, under 18 years old unless the person is shown in an incidental role in a natural situation and where there is no implication they will engage in gambling activities (e.g., depicting a family dinner).
o Featuring anyone who is, or seems to be, under 25 years old participating in gambling.
o Depicting content that uses or contains cartoon figures, puppets, animatronics, mascots
or animation that would likely be appealing to children or young people.
o Depicting characters that are or are similar to 'cuddly’ or ‘cute’ animals, common fairy talessuch as Little Red Riding Hood or Hansel and Gretel, characters popular with people under 18 years old (e.g., princesses, pirates, Bluey), and cultural characters such as Santa
Claus, the Tooth Fairy, and the Easter Bunny.
o Content similar to video games and eSports popular with people under 18 years old.
o Depicting toys, play scenes, or playgrounds.
• Advertisements must not cause, condone, or encourage extended, continuous, excessive and impulsive play. Examples of content that would be prohibited under this requirement include, but are not limited to:
o Portraying gambling as a means of relieving or improving a person’s financial, professional or personal difficulties.
o Stating or implying a link between gambling and sexual or relationship success, or enhanced attractiveness.
o Suggesting that gambling can enhance personal qualities; for example, that it can improve self-image or self-esteem, or is a way to gain control, superiority, recognition or admiration.
o Portraying, condoning or encouraging peer pressure.
o Portraying gambling as indispensable or as a priority in life.
o Suggesting gambling is a rite of passage.
o Linking gambling to toughness, resilience and recklessness.
o Condoning or featuring gambling in a working environment.
o Encouraging excessive participation (beyond a player’s means).
o Portraying, condoning or encouraging gambling in combination with the consumption of alcohol.
o Promoting gambling as an alternative to employment, as a financial investment, or as a requirement for financial security.
o Encouraging play as a means of recovering past gambling or other financial losses.
o Suggesting that gambling can provide an escape from personal or professional problems;e.g., loneliness or depression.
• Advertisements must not include the expressions “Win” or “$” unless these expressions specifically relate to a prize that has been determined or is payable, or to an estimate of a prize that can be won.
• Advertisements must be truthful and shall not mislead players or misrepresent products. Examples of content that would be prohibited under this requirement include, but are not limited to:
o Making claims related to winning or the prizes that can be won that are not based on fact, are unable to be proven, or are exaggerated.
o Implying that the chances of winning increase the longer one plays or the more one spends.
o Suggesting that a player’s skill can influence the outcome of gambling activity in relation to gambling where a player’s skill cannot influence the outcome.
o Exaggerating the extent to which skill can influence the outcome of gambling activity in relation to gambling where a player’s skill can influence the outcome.
o Misrepresenting the level of financial risk.
o Creating a false sense of urgency such that consumers may be misled into thinking they must act quickly to participate or win.
• Advertisements must not play on people’s fears, beliefs or superstitions or use cultural symbols, names or imagery likely to cause serious or widespread offence.
• Advertisements must not include images or sounds suggestive of:
o Coins being inserted or dispensed from a gaming machine.
o Banknotes being inserted into or dispensed from a gaming machine or automated table game equipment.
o Tickets being printed or dispensed from a gaming machine or automated table game equipment.
o Poker chips; or
o Electronic Gaming Machine (slots) or jackpot winnings.
• Advertisements cannot refer to the benefit from the community grants that occurs as a result of the online casino gambling duty that is paid.
Harm Minimisation Provisions in the Bill
• The minimum age for online gambling will be 18 years. The provider must take all reasonable steps to ensure players are at least 18 years old. Age verification must occur before the player is permitted to make a deposit.
• An identification verification system must be in place that reliably verifies the player’s full name, date of birth, address, and self-exclusion status. In practice, the self-exclusion verification requirement is likely to require providers to cross-check whether a player’s 
identity matches that of a person who has previously self-excluded with them, and/or asking players to disclose whether they have previously self-excluded as part of the account creation process.
• It will be a criminal offence for anyone to place a bet on behalf of a person who is underage, with a maximum penalty of a fine up to $10,000.00, with an available defence if the person had reasonable grounds to believe the person was 18 years of age or over.
• The provider must not offer or provide credit for gambling.
• The provider must take all reasonable steps to ensure the risk of harm from online casino gambling is minimised and the harm minimisation regulations are followed.
• The provider must exclude from gambling persons who self-exclude and persons who are identified as problem gamblers.
• A register of persons who wish to be excluded from online casino gambling will be implemented from 1 December 2027. The costs of administering the register will be recovered from providers.
• The provider must have policies to monitor, identify and assist people experiencing gambling harm. The information providers will be required to collect about player behaviour will be determined by the Regulator.
Proposed Regulations on Harm Minimisation
• Providers must provide players with options to set limits on their gambling, including limits based on time, deposits and spending across different time periods and stake levels.
• Providers must offer players the ability to self-exclude themselves. The process for selfexclusion must be easy to identify and to complete.
• Providers must participate in a centralised self-exclusion register, if an appropriate register is made available.
• Providers must offer players the ability to set the frequency of breaks in their play, together with the frequency of personalised pop-up alerts (which must show key information about their current gambling session).
• Providers must have policies to monitor, identify and assist people experiencing gambling harm. The information required to be collected about player behaviour will be determined by the Regulator.
• Providers must include harm minimisation messaging in all online casino gambling advertisements. The content and technical specifications for messages will be determined by the Regulator.
• Game designs and user interface features must be designed to prevent extended, continuous and impulsive play, including by prohibiting the use of autoplay.
• Network progressive jackpots must only be linked between branded online casinos that are controlled by licensed providers.
• Players are limited to playing one online slot game on a platform at any one time.
Consumer Protection Provisions in the Bill
• A formal consumer complaints process will be established:
o A provider must have a complaints process, handle complaints within 40 working days of being received, publicise that information on their website, and keep a complaints register.
o The provider must provide the Regulator, every three months, with details about the complaints it has received and how they have been handled.
• Complaints may also be made by players direct to the Regulator.
Proposed Regulations on Consumer Protection
• Providers must retain all records related to a player’s account information until seven years after the business relationship ends.
• Providers are to collect and verify a player’s full name and date of birth at the account creation stage before an account holder can make a deposit, and determine whether the person has self-excluded themselves from gambling.
• Providers are to prohibit the use of credit contracts to pay for online gambling, including the use of credit cards and buy now pay later facilities. Providers are permitted to accept any other form of payment they choose to offer; e.g., they can choose to accept cryptocurrency.
• Players shall only be able to hold one account per platform, must register a deposit method at the account creation stage, and may only register one deposit method at any one time.
• Prior to placing a bet, consumers must be provided with sufficient information to make an informed decision. Information on their current player account balance and playing history should be easily accessible on the platform.
• Game designs and user interface features must be clear and not mislead the player. Games shall remove and add credits accurately and according to game rules, and credit a player’s account completely and immediately on winning.
• Providers must provide information and services in languages commonly spoken in New Zealand and must communicate in a form, language and manner that enables the consumer to understand the information provided.
• Providers must prevent collusion, cheating, and software programs that automatically participate in game play (automated gambling).
• Player funds must be protected and available for withdrawal on request. Players cannot be able to reverse pending withdrawals once a withdrawal request has been made.
• A player may only hold one account per platform, and a player must be able to close an account and withdraw remaining funds without undue delay.
Enforcement Provisions in the Bill
• The Regulator will have the ability to suspend or cancel a licence. The Regulator will have a range of enforcement tools, including formal warnings, enforceable undertakings, takedown notices, and pecuniary penalties.
• It will be an offence to offer online casino gambling in New Zealand without a licence, with a penalty of up to $300,000 for an individual, and $5m for a body corporate or partnership
• It will be a breach to advertise, sponsor or promote online casino gambling in New Zealand without a licence, with a penalty of up to $300,000 for an individual and $5m for a body corporate or partnership. This will apply from 1 May 2026.
• From 1 May 2026, the Regulator will have the ability to issue take-down notices. Takedown notices must be complied with within two days. It will be a breach of the legislation for an unlicensed provider to fail to comply with a take-down notice issued by the Regulator, 
with a penalty of up to $300,000 for an individual and $5m for a body corporate or partnership.
• A monetary penalty can be imposed simultaneously on both the provider, and the provider’s directors and/or management staff.
• A public register will be kept, which will include a summary of any enforcement action taken against a licensed provider.
• The Regulator will have the power to require anyone to provide information to it if the Regulator considers it necessary or desirable for the purpose of performing or exercising the Regulator’s duties.
• The Online Casino Gambling Bill empowers active co-operation by the Regulator, and information sharing with equivalent overseas regulators, which may assist the New Zealand Regulator and the overseas regulators.
Anti-Money Laundering
• Online casino providers will fall within the definition of a reporting entity under the AntiMoney Laundering and Countering Financing of Terrorism Act 2009.
Key Person Notifications
• Providers must notify the Regulator before a new key officer is employed or engaged.

## Market Share and Estimated Online Gambling Spend
In October 2025, TAB New Zealand estimated that the annual online spend with offshore gambling websites by New Zealanders was $700-$800m.
For the year ending 30 June 2025, 26 online gambling providers were registered with Inland Revenue for offshore gambling duty. The duty excludes amounts from betting on sports and racing, so it is a more accurate baseline for market size than GST data. The reported gross 
gambling revenue liable for offshore gambling duty was $530.8m for the 12 months to 30 June 2025. Fifteen of the 26 reporting entities contributed 99.8% of the total gross revenue. The top five entities generated 89.6% of the total gross gambling revenue. The Regulator estimates growth of approximately 3% year-over-year.

generated by licensed providers will be. Factors that will influence future 
revenue, especially during the market establishment phase, include:
• whether the top 15 entities currently paying the duty will be successful in 
obtaining an online casino gambling licence;
• how many of the top 15 entities are currently operating multiple brands; 
and
• how regulatory settings (e.g., restrictions on advertising) will influence 
revenue and market growth. Cabinet decisions on these settings have not yet 
been made.
With those factors in mind, we have assumed that licensed providers’ GGR is 
likely to fall within the range of $330 million to $440 million in the year ending 
June 2027 during the market’s establishment phase. Our colleagues from Inland 
Revenue think that a $520 million estimate is more likely, which is closer to the 
current GGR in an unregulated market. Therefore, we have considered a wide 
range to account for this
We also consider that GGR is likely to increase in future years as the online 
casino market continues to grow and New Zealand licenced providers establish 
market share. The Department estimates growth of approximately 3% yearover-year.
